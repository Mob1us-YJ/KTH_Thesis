\begin{scontents}[store-env=abstracts,print-env=true]
Wi-Fi sensing leverages Channel State Information (CSI) extracted from commodity Wi-Fi hardware to detect and interpret physical activities without requiring users to carry any devices. Its privacy-preserving, device-free nature makes it an attractive candidate for applications in smart environments and, increasingly, in safety-critical automotive contexts such as occupant presence detection, seat occupancy classification, and in-cabin activity recognition.

Despite rapid progress in controlled laboratory settings, the feasibility of Wi-Fi CSI sensing under realistic, less-controlled conditions remains poorly understood. Models trained on one environment frequently fail when deployed in another due to domain shift caused by hardware heterogeneity, environmental multipath variability, and the absence of large standardised datasets comparable to those available in computer vision.

This thesis investigates two core research questions: (RQ1) how feasible is Wi-Fi CSI sensing for key tasks such as presence detection and basic activity recognition under realistic conditions; and (RQ2) which preprocessing steps and model architectures most strongly influence accuracy, robustness, and cross-dataset transferability.

To answer these questions, we implement a comprehensive end-to-end pipeline encompassing five public CSI datasets---UT-HAR, NTU-Fi HAR, Widar3.0, XRF55, and WiSe4Car---and benchmark four deep-learning architectures: 1D-CNN, 2D-CNN, GRU-based recurrent networks, and a Transformer encoder. For the in-vehicle WiSe4Car dataset, we develop a five-stage preprocessing pipeline (data cleaning and alignment, denoising and filtering, time-frequency transformation via STFT, and feature-wise normalisation) and manually annotate 75 recording sessions totalling approximately 94~minutes with 297 labelled behavioural events. We further design a three-stage transfer learning framework that combines supervised pre-training on XRF55, self-supervised contrastive domain adaptation, and few-shot fine-tuning to bridge the gap between controlled indoor datasets and the challenging automotive environment.

Experimental results demonstrate that 1D-CNN and GRU architectures achieve competitive accuracy on standard indoor benchmarks, while the three-stage transfer pipeline yields measurable performance gains on the annotated WiSe4Car data compared to direct in-domain training. The study highlights that preprocessing choices---particularly phase sanitisation, windowing strategy, and time-frequency transformation---have an outsized impact on cross-environment performance, often exceeding the contribution of model architecture selection.

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}
%% ───────────────────────────────────────

The proliferation of wireless local area network infrastructure has created an unexpected opportunity for pervasive, device-free sensing. Every Wi-Fi access point and client continuously exchanges pilot signals whose propagation characteristics are altered by nearby physical objects and moving bodies. Rather than discarding this environmental information, researchers have proposed harvesting it---transforming commodity Wi-Fi hardware into a passive sensor that requires no user-worn devices, no additional infrastructure, and no line-of-sight access~\cite{ma2019wifi}. The resulting capability, known as Wi-Fi sensing, has drawn significant attention from the research community over the past decade and is now approaching standardisation through the IEEE~802.11bf Task Group~\cite{du2025overview}.

\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:intro-background}

The fundamental sensing signal is Channel State Information (CSI): per-subcarrier channel estimates made available by modern Wi-Fi chipsets as a by-product of OFDM demodulation. Under the IEEE~802.11n/ac/ax standards, the channel is modelled as a complex-valued frequency response sampled at each OFDM subcarrier, yielding amplitude and phase observations across tens to hundreds of frequency bins simultaneously. This stands in sharp contrast to Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI), which collapses the entire channel into a single scalar power measurement. As Yang~et~al.~\cite{yang2013from} demonstrated, the subcarrier-level resolution of CSI enables detection of subtle body movements that are imperceptible to RSSI-based systems.

When a human body enters the propagation environment, the reflected and diffracted signal components are perturbed in ways that depend on the person's position, posture, and motion. These perturbations produce distinctive temporal and spectral patterns in the CSI stream~\cite{wilson2010radio,wang2015understanding}. By modelling these patterns with machine learning, a wide range of sensing tasks becomes feasible without requiring any user participation. Users carry no wearable sensors; the sensing system operates passively and continuously in the background.

Practical CSI extraction has been enabled by open tools such as Nexmon CSI~\cite{gringoli2019free}, which exposes subcarrier-level measurements from commodity Broadcom/Cypress chipsets. Combined with Intel 5300 NIC patches and Atheros-based extractors, these tools have seeded a growing ecosystem of public CSI datasets and reproducible experimental infrastructure~\cite{yang2023sensefi}. Comprehensive surveys by Ma~et~al.~\cite{ma2019wifi}, Yousefi~et~al.~\cite{yousefi2017survey}, and Wu~et~al.~\cite{wu2017device} document the field's evolution from pattern-based recognition with handcrafted features toward deep-learning and physics-inspired model-based approaches, while more recent surveys~\cite{wang2026survey,radwan2026ssl} examine the emerging challenges of cross-domain generalisation and self-supervised representation learning.

Wi-Fi CSI sensing has demonstrated feasibility across a broad application space. Human Activity Recognition (HAR)---identifying activities such as walking, sitting, running, and falling from CSI time-series---has become the canonical benchmark task~\cite{yang2018device,wang2014e,chen2018wifi}. Beyond coarse activity categories, researchers have demonstrated fine-grained gesture recognition~\cite{yang2019learning,tan2022enabling,gu2022wigrunt}, vital sign monitoring~\cite{gu2019wifi}, fall detection~\cite{chu2023deep}, crowd counting~\cite{zou2017freecount}, and even body pose estimation~\cite{yang2022metafi} and person re-identification~\cite{zou2018wifi}. The SenseFi benchmark library~\cite{yang2023sensefi} consolidates many of these tasks under a unified deep-learning framework, enabling direct architecture comparisons across standardised datasets. Despite high reported accuracy in controlled laboratory settings---often exceeding 95\% on standard indoor datasets---a comprehensive survey by Wang~et~al.~\cite{wang2026survey} reviewing over 200 papers identifies device heterogeneity, human body shape diversity, and environmental diversity as the three root causes of the persistent deployment gap, showing that no single existing technique fully resolves all three simultaneously.

The automotive domain presents a compelling but largely unexplored opportunity for Wi-Fi sensing. Modern vehicles are increasingly equipped with IEEE~802.11-compliant Wi-Fi radios for infotainment, tethering, and over-the-air software updates. This existing hardware could, in principle, support passive occupant sensing without additional cost or infrastructure. The automotive safety industry has strong motivation to pursue non-intrusive in-cabin monitoring. Child presence detection (CPD) is a high-priority safety function: an estimated hundreds of children die each year in vehicles due to hyperthermia after being left unattended. In response, the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UN ECE) regulations and the European General Safety Regulation (EU~2019/2144) are progressively mandating automatic CPD systems in new vehicles from 2025 onwards. Wi-Fi CSI-based sensing offers a privacy-preserving alternative: unlike camera-based systems, it does not capture identifiable images; unlike pressure sensors, it can detect an occupant not in contact with a seat surface; and unlike dedicated radar hardware, it leverages the existing telematics radio.

Prior work has demonstrated the technical feasibility of Wi-Fi-based in-cabin monitoring across several tasks. WiDrive~\cite{bai2019widrive} introduced the first real-time in-car driver activity recognition system, achieving 91.3\% average accuracy on seven takeover-relevant activities. CARIN~\cite{bai2020carin} extended this to realistic multi-occupant vehicles, achieving an F1 score of 90.9\% on over 3{,}000 real-car traces. DeepCPD~\cite{jayaweera2025deepcpd} demonstrated 92.86\% overall CPD accuracy across 30 vehicle models and over 500~hours of data. Zuo~et~al.~\cite{zuo2024invehicle} achieved 95.54\% in-vehicle gesture recognition using a Transformer--LSTM fusion architecture on only two Wi-Fi receivers. However, none of these systems address the cross-domain generalisation problem from public indoor datasets to the automotive environment---the central challenge of this work.

\section{Problem}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Original Problem and Definition}
\label{sec:problem-def}

This thesis investigates the feasibility of Wi-Fi CSI sensing for automotive in-cabin applications, with Autoliv Research as the industrial partner. The core problem is the \emph{domain gap}: the automotive cabin is a profoundly different sensing environment from the controlled indoor rooms in which existing models are trained. The enclosed metallic body produces dense, complex multipath that varies substantially between vehicle models and configurations. Occupants are constrained to fixed seating positions---producing activity patterns quite different from open-room scenarios---and hardware-specific phase offsets in the Intel Wi-Fi~6 adapter used by the WiSe4Car dataset~\cite{markert2025wise4car} add further pre-processing complexity. No large-scale, annotated automotive CSI dataset exists that would enable training models from scratch, making transfer from public indoor corpora the only viable path.

Three interrelated challenges motivate the research agenda:

\paragraph{Domain shift.}
Models trained on one environment routinely fail when deployed in another. The root causes are hardware-induced phase offsets (Carrier Frequency Offset, Sampling Frequency Offset), environment-specific multipath fingerprints, and differences in subject anthropometry and movement style~\cite{zou2018robust,zhang2018crosssense}. Bridging this gap---from controlled indoor datasets to the automotive environment---is the central technical challenge of this work~\cite{yang2020mobileda,bu2021transfersense,wang2026survey}.

\paragraph{Dataset scarcity and annotation difficulty.}
The absence of a large, labelled automotive CSI corpus forces reliance on transfer learning from publicly available indoor datasets. The WiSe4Car dataset~\cite{markert2025wise4car}, released in 2025, is the first publicly available CSI dataset collected in real vehicle cabins, but it was originally collected without behavioural activity labels suitable for HAR evaluation. Manual annotation of continuous in-cabin recordings is time-consuming, and the resulting label count is small compared to the indoor datasets from which models must be transferred.

\paragraph{Preprocessing sensitivity.}
Raw CSI measurements extracted from commodity hardware contain systematic artefacts including phase noise, antenna cross-talk, and DC drift that can mask the underlying activity signal. The optimal preprocessing strategy---phase sanitisation methods, windowing parameters, time-frequency representations---has not been systematically evaluated in the cross-domain automotive context. Evidence from indoor benchmarks suggests that preprocessing choices can affect accuracy as much or more than model architecture selection~\cite{zhang2021widar3,yang2023sensefi}.

\subsection{Research Questions}
\label{sec:rq}

The challenges described above give rise to two core research questions:

\begin{description}
  \item[RQ1] \textit{Feasibility under realistic conditions.} Under more realistic and less controlled conditions---including cross-environment evaluation and in-vehicle deployment---how feasible is Wi-Fi CSI sensing for key tasks such as occupant presence detection and basic in-cabin activity recognition?
  \item[RQ2] \textit{Design choices that matter.} Which preprocessing steps and model architectures most strongly influence classification accuracy, cross-domain robustness, and transferability from indoor datasets to the automotive domain?
\end{description}

\section{Purpose and Goals}
\label{sec:goals}

\subsection{Goals and Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

The main contributions of this thesis are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Comprehensive indoor benchmark.} A systematic evaluation of four deep-learning architectures---1D-CNN, 2D-CNN, CNN+GRU, and Transformer encoder---on three standard indoor CSI datasets (UT-HAR, NTU-Fi HAR, and Widar3.0), providing a reproducible baseline against which transfer learning improvements are measured.

  \item \textbf{Automotive preprocessing pipeline.} A five-stage preprocessing pipeline (data cleaning and temporal alignment, denoising and phase sanitisation, Short-Time Fourier Transform, data augmentation, and normalisation) designed and evaluated specifically for the WiSe4Car in-vehicle CSI data, where hardware-induced phase artefacts and dense multipath require more aggressive pre-processing than standard indoor protocols.

  \item \textbf{WiSe4Car behavioural annotation.} A manual annotation of 75~WiSe4Car recording sessions totalling 5631~seconds ($\approx$94~minutes), producing 297~labelled behavioural events across entry/exit, posture change, reaching, and idle categories. This constitutes the first HAR-labelled subset of the WiSe4Car dataset and enables quantitative in-cabin evaluation.

  \item \textbf{Three-stage transfer learning framework.} A pipeline combining supervised pre-training on the XRF55~\cite{wang2024xrf55} indoor dataset, self-supervised contrastive domain adaptation on unlabelled WiSe4Car recordings, and few-shot fine-tuning on the annotated in-cabin subset. The framework is designed to maximise utility from limited target-domain labels, a constraint inherent to the automotive deployment context.

  \item \textbf{Empirical analysis of design choices.} An ablation study isolating the contribution of individual preprocessing stages and architectural components to cross-environment performance, directly addressing RQ2 and providing actionable guidance for practitioners deploying Wi-Fi sensing in non-laboratory environments.
\end{itemize}

\section{Delimitations}
\label{sec:delimitations}

This study is conducted exclusively with public CSI datasets; no new Wi-Fi measurements are collected. The automotive context is addressed through the WiSe4Car dataset~\cite{markert2025wise4car} together with manual annotations contributed by this thesis. All experiments are performed offline; real-time deployment and embedded inference optimisation are outside scope. The study covers the 2.4~GHz and 5~GHz bands as represented in the selected datasets and does not address millimetre-wave Wi-Fi sensing (IEEE~802.11ad/ay). Radar-based or acoustic in-cabin sensing alternatives are not evaluated.

\section{Wi-Fi Channel State Information}
\label{sec:csi}

\subsection{OFDM Physical Layer and CSI Acquisition}
\label{sec:csi-ofdm}

Modern Wi-Fi systems operating under the IEEE~802.11n/ac/ax standards employ Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing (OFDM), a multicarrier modulation technique that divides the available channel bandwidth into a set of narrowband orthogonal subcarriers. During reception, the chipset estimates the complex channel transfer function on each subcarrier as part of the normal OFDM demodulation process; this per-subcarrier estimate is the Channel State Information (CSI). Tools such as Nexmon CSI~\cite{gringoli2019free} expose these measurements to user-space applications by patching the device driver, enabling passive sensing without any modification to the transmitting device.

\subsection{CSI Signal Model}
\label{sec:csi-model}

For a system with \(N_T\) transmit and \(N_R\) receive antennas and \(S\) OFDM subcarriers, the raw CSI measurement at time \(t\) is a complex-valued tensor \(H_t \in \mathbb{C}^{N_T \times N_R \times S}\). Each element \(H_t[i,j,k]\) encodes the amplitude attenuation and phase rotation experienced by the carrier on the \(k\)-th subcarrier of the path from transmit antenna \(i\) to receive antenna \(j\):
\[
  H_t[i,j,k] = \sum_{\ell} a_\ell \, e^{-j 2\pi f_k \tau_\ell},
\]
where the sum runs over all multipath components, \(a_\ell\) is the complex amplitude of path~\(\ell\), \(\tau_\ell\) is its propagation delay, and \(f_k\) is the subcarrier frequency. When a human body is present, it induces time-varying perturbations in \(H_t\): the body scatters and absorbs signal energy, altering both the amplitude and phase of individual multipath components. These perturbations produce distinctive temporal and spectral patterns across the subcarrier dimension~\cite{wilson2010radio,wang2015understanding}, which machine-learning models can exploit for activity classification. Figure~\ref{fig:csi_principle} illustrates this sensing mechanism.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/Modeling_Turning_Incar.png}
  \caption{Wi-Fi CSI sensing principle. (a)~A transmitter and receiver exchange signals over a line-of-sight (LoS) path and multiple reflected paths; a human body introduces an additional dynamic multipath component $\vec{H}_d$ that varies with movement. (b)~Phasor representation: the total channel $\vec{H}$ is the vector sum of the static component $\vec{H}_s$ and $\vec{H}_d$; as the person moves, $\vec{H}$ traces an arc between constructive ($P_B$) and destructive ($P_A$) interference states. (c)~The resulting CSI amplitude over time oscillates between $|\vec{H}_s|+|\vec{H}_d|$ and $|\vec{H}_s|-|\vec{H}_d|$, producing the characteristic fluctuation exploited by activity recognition algorithms.}
  \label{fig:csi_principle}
\end{figure}

The key advantage of CSI over Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI) is its subcarrier-level resolution~\cite{yang2013from}: while RSSI collapses the entire channel to a single scalar power measurement, CSI preserves the full multipath fingerprint across tens to hundreds of subcarriers, enabling detection of subtle body movements that are invisible to RSSI-based systems.

\subsection{Phase Artefacts and Sanitisation}
\label{sec:csi-phase}

The raw CSI phase is corrupted by hardware-induced offsets. Carrier Frequency Offset (CFO) between the transmitter and receiver oscillators introduces a time-varying linear phase ramp across subcarriers. Sampling Frequency Offset (SFO) produces a similar but smaller distortion. Both artefacts can introduce phase errors that exceed the body-motion signal in magnitude, rendering the raw phase unusable for sensing~\cite{yousefi2017survey}. The standard remediation is \emph{conjugate multiplication}: computing \(H_t[\cdot,\cdot,k] \cdot H_t^{*}[\cdot,\cdot,\text{ref}]\) with a fixed reference subcarrier to cancel the common phase offset. The resulting sanitised phase is relative rather than absolute, but is stable enough for activity discrimination. For the Intel Wi-Fi~6 adapter used in WiSe4Car, additional DC-bias removal and linear-trend subtraction are required before any learned feature extractor can be applied.

A foundational taxonomy of Wi-Fi sensing approaches was established by Wu~et~al.~\cite{wu2017device}, who distinguish \emph{pattern-based} methods---which learn discriminative CSI signatures using statistical features and classical machine learning---from \emph{model-based} methods that leverage Fresnel zone propagation physics to achieve finer-grained body tracking without requiring extensive labelled training data. Ma~et~al.~\cite{ma2019wifi} offer the most comprehensive survey of the field through 2018, covering 29 sensing tasks and systematically analysing hardware platforms, signal processing pipelines, and machine learning architectures.

\section{Applications of Wi-Fi CSI Sensing}
\label{sec:applications}

Wi-Fi CSI sensing has demonstrated feasibility across a broad application space. Human Activity Recognition (HAR)---identifying activities such as walking, sitting, running, and falling from CSI time-series~\cite{yang2018device,wang2015understanding}---has become the canonical benchmark task. Beyond coarse activity categories, researchers have demonstrated fine-grained gesture recognition~\cite{yang2019learning,tan2022enabling,gu2022wigrunt}, vital sign monitoring~\cite{gu2019wifi}, fall detection~\cite{chu2023deep}, crowd counting~\cite{zou2017freecount}, and person re-identification~\cite{zou2018wifi}.

The XRF55 benchmark~\cite{wang2024xrf55} represents a significant expansion of the evaluation space, providing 42.9K RF samples across 55~action classes from a multi-modal platform (Wi-Fi, RFID, mmWave radar, Azure Kinect), covering human--object interactions, fitness activities, and human--computer interactions from 39~subjects. For multi-occupant scenarios, WiMANS~\cite{huang2024wimans} introduced the first dual-band (2.4/5~GHz) benchmark dataset capturing the simultaneous activities of up to five users, with 11{,}286 CSI samples annotated for user identity, location, and activity---highlighting the fundamental complexity gap between single-user laboratory studies and real-world deployments.

In automotive contexts, the relevant tasks shift toward occupant presence detection and in-cabin activity recognition---capabilities that could directly support safety system configuration and child presence detection without requiring cameras~\cite{markert2025wise4car,bai2019widrive,bai2020carin,jia2020wifind,jayaweera2025deepcpd}. Section~\ref{sec:incabin} reviews this line of work in detail.

\section{Deep Learning Architectures for CSI Sensing}
\label{sec:models}

Early systems relied on handcrafted features---Doppler velocity profiles, wavelet coefficients, and empirical mode decomposition---fed into support vector machines or shallow neural networks~\cite{wang2014e}. The shift to end-to-end deep learning, reviewed comprehensively in SenseFi~\cite{yang2023sensefi}, has yielded substantial accuracy improvements by learning directly from raw CSI amplitude or phase matrices.

\subsection{1D Convolutional Networks}
\label{sec:models-1dcnn}

1D-CNN architectures apply temporal convolutions directly to raw CSI amplitude streams. Two or more stacked \texttt{Conv1D} layers with ReLU activations and max pooling extract local temporal patterns, followed by global average pooling and a fully connected classification head. The low parameter count makes 1D-CNNs computationally efficient baselines suitable for real-time on-device inference.

\subsection{2D Convolutional Networks and Spectrogram Representations}
\label{sec:models-2dcnn}

2D-CNN models treat the subcarrier-time CSI matrix as a spatial image, applying multi-scale convolutional backbones with residual connections to extract both spectral and temporal features simultaneously. A common variant first transforms the raw CSI amplitude into a Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) spectrogram, treating the resulting frequency-time tile as a two-channel input (amplitude and phase), which allows the model to benefit from standard image-classification pre-training techniques~\cite{elzein2024csi}.

\subsection{Hybrid Recurrent Architectures}
\label{sec:models-gru}

Hybrid CNN+GRU architectures~\cite{wakili2025evaluating} combine the local feature extraction capability of convolutional layers with the long-range temporal modelling capacity of Gated Recurrent Units. Two \texttt{Conv1D} layers first extract short-term patterns, and the resulting sequence is fed to a GRU layer that captures inter-window temporal dependencies. Dropout applied after each recurrent layer regularises the model against overfitting on small datasets---a key consideration in the WiSe4Car target domain.

\subsection{Transformer Encoders}
\label{sec:models-transformer}

Transformer encoders with positional embeddings apply multi-head self-attention over windowed CSI sequences, directly modelling inter-subcarrier correlations at all temporal lags simultaneously~\cite{yang2023sensefi}. While Transformers have shown competitive or superior performance on larger benchmark datasets, they require substantially more labelled data to reach their accuracy ceiling than convolutional architectures, making them less practical for the small WiSe4Car target-domain subset.

\section{Cross-Domain Generalisation}
\label{sec:crossdomain}

A primary challenge in learning-based Wi-Fi sensing is environmental dependency~\cite{zou2018robust,wang2026survey}: models trained in one environment frequently degrade in a different setting. A recent comprehensive survey by Wang~et~al.~\cite{wang2026survey} reviews over 200 papers published since 2015, organising generalisation techniques along four pipeline stages---experimental setup, signal preprocessing, feature learning, and model deployment.

\subsection{Root Causes of Domain Shift}
\label{sec:crossdomain-causes}

Three root causes of domain shift are consistently identified in the literature:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Device heterogeneity.} Hardware-specific Carrier Frequency Offset (CFO) and Sampling Frequency Offset (SFO) introduce phase offsets that differ between chipsets, making raw CSI phase non-transferable across devices~\cite{yousefi2017survey}.
  \item \textbf{Environmental diversity.} Room geometry, wall materials, and furniture placement determine the multipath structure, which acts as an environment-specific fingerprint that shifts when the model is deployed in a new location~\cite{zhang2018crosssense}.
  \item \textbf{Human body diversity.} Subject height, weight, and movement style alter the amplitude and Doppler signature of a given activity, causing person-specific variation that challenges models trained on a fixed subject pool~\cite{zou2018robust}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Generalisation Approaches}
\label{sec:crossdomain-methods}

Several complementary strategies have been proposed to address domain shift:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Adversarial domain adaptation}~\cite{zou2018robust,yang2020mobileda}: a domain discriminator with reversed gradient encourages domain-invariant feature representations that are indistinguishable across source and target environments.
  \item \textbf{Zero-shot transfer}: Widar3.0~\cite{zhang2021widar3} uses a body-coordinate-velocity (BVP) representation that is invariant to transmitter/receiver geometry, enabling generalisation to new rooms and subject positions without retraining.
  \item \textbf{Few-shot calibration}: TransferSense~\cite{bu2021transfersense} and CrossSense~\cite{zhang2018crosssense} demonstrate adaptation to new environments with minimal labelled target samples, showing that only a handful of calibration examples can substantially recover domain-shifted accuracy.
  \item \textbf{Self-supervised pre-training}: AutoFi~\cite{yang2022autofi} uses geometric self-supervised objectives to pre-train a feature extractor without labels, then fine-tunes on small labelled sets. A comprehensive survey by Radwan~et~al.~\cite{radwan2026ssl} systematically compares contrastive methods (SimCLR, MoCo, VICReg) and non-contrastive alternatives (BYOL, SimSiam, Barlow Twins) for Wi-Fi sensing, showing that SSL pre-training provides consistent cross-domain accuracy improvements when labelled target data is scarce.
\end{itemize}

\section{Automotive In-Cabin Wi-Fi Sensing}
\label{sec:incabin}

Only recently has systematic research attention turned to the automotive domain. The unique challenges of vehicle cabins---enclosed metallic geometry, dense and rapidly changing multipath, close-range occupants constrained to fixed seating positions, and engine-induced structural vibration---demand approaches specifically designed for this environment rather than direct transfer from open-room indoor sensing systems.

\subsection{Sensing Tasks and Prior Work}
\label{sec:incabin-tasks}

\textbf{Driver activity recognition.}
WiDrive~\cite{bai2019widrive} introduced the first real-time in-car driver activity recognition system, targeting the safety-critical scenario of autonomous vehicle takeover. WiDrive extracts fine-grained body velocity profiles from CSI collected by commodity Wi-Fi cards and recognises seven small-scale driver activities with 91.3\% average accuracy. Crucially, it incorporates an online Expectation-Maximisation adaptation mechanism that updates the recognition model for new drivers and vehicle types without offline retraining. CARIN~\cite{bai2020carin} extended this paradigm to the realistic multi-passenger case, achieving an F1 score of 90.9\% on over 3{,}000 real-car traces, outperforming three prior single-occupancy baselines by a margin of 32.2\%.

\textbf{Driver fatigue and vital sign monitoring.}
WiFind~\cite{jia2020wifind} applies Hilbert--Huang Transform decomposition to CSI amplitude streams, extracting both motion-based and respiration-based features indicative of fatigue onset, and achieves 89.6\% detection accuracy in real driving environments with a false positive rate below 10\%. V$^2$iFi~\cite{zheng2020v2ifi} addressed multi-passenger vital sign monitoring using a compact ultra-wideband impulse radio module, distinguishing multiple occupants' respiratory and cardiac signals by relative seating position. Ibrahim and Brown~\cite{ibrahim2021vehicle} provided an early feasibility demonstration of real-time breathing rate monitoring in a stationary vehicle using commodity Nexmon CSI extraction, validating that standard off-the-shelf Wi-Fi hardware suffices for contactless vital sign sensing.

\textbf{Child presence detection.}
For the mandated safety task of child presence detection (CPD), DeepCPD~\cite{jayaweera2025deepcpd} proposes a deep learning framework that uses the autocorrelation function (ACF) of CSI as an environment-independent feature to capture spatio-temporal motion patterns while suppressing static reflections that vary across vehicle models. Evaluated across 30 vehicle models and over 500~hours of recordings, DeepCPD achieves 92.86\% overall accuracy with a 91.45\% per-child detection rate and only 6.14\% false alarm rate.

\textbf{Gesture recognition.}
Zuo~et~al.~\cite{zuo2024invehicle} investigated in-vehicle CSI gesture recognition using a reduced two-receiver setup. Their FEDRT-LSTM model---a Transformer-based feature extraction module combined with an LSTM temporal classifier---achieves 95.54\% accuracy on four driver gestures using body velocity profile data.

\subsection{The WiSe4Car Dataset}
\label{sec:wisedataset}

The WiSe4Car dataset~\cite{markert2025wise4car}, released in 2025, is the first publicly available Wi-Fi CSI dataset collected inside standard vehicle cabins using a commercial Intel Wi-Fi~6 adapter with 256~subcarriers. WiSe4Car covers multiple passengers and driving scenarios and was originally collected for passenger height and weight estimation, without the behavioural activity labels required for HAR evaluation. This gap---a publicly available automotive CSI corpus without HAR annotations---is a central motivation for the annotation and transfer learning contributions of this thesis. The particular phase-offset characteristics of the Intel Wi-Fi~6 chipset introduce pre-processing requirements that differ from those of the Nexmon-compatible Broadcom/Atheros hardware used in most public indoor CSI datasets, presenting an additional challenge for cross-domain transfer.

\section{Summary}

Wi-Fi CSI sensing offers a compelling combination of device-free operation, fine-grained sensing capability, and privacy preservation. Translating laboratory-level accuracy to realistic deployments requires addressing domain shift, dataset scarcity, and annotation challenges. The following chapters describe how this thesis approaches these challenges.

This chapter describes the methodology used to evaluate the feasibility of Wi-Fi CSI sensing under cross-domain and automotive conditions. \section{Methodological Overview}
\label{sec:overview}

\subsection{Research Design}

The central question driving the experimental design is: \textit{how can a reproducible pipeline be constructed to evaluate Wi-Fi CSI sensing feasibility under realistic automotive domain shift?} Two constraints shape the answer. First, no large-scale annotated in-vehicle CSI dataset exists; WiSe4Car is publicly available but was not designed for activity recognition and contains no behavioural labels. Second, the domain gap between existing indoor CSI benchmarks and vehicle-cabin recordings is substantial---driven by hardware heterogeneity, enclosed multipath environments, and the absence of a shared preprocessing standard across datasets.

The methodology therefore proceeds from controlled to realistic conditions: indoor benchmarks establish a robust baseline and identify a stable backbone; automotive dataset construction converts WiSe4Car into a usable target domain; and transfer learning experiments quantify how much indoor knowledge can be reused in the automotive setting.

\subsection{Training Protocol}
\label{sec:trainprotocol}

All Stage~I experiments follow a uniform protocol to ensure comparability:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Optimiser.} Adam~\cite{kingma2015adam} at initial learning rate $10^{-3}$ with cosine annealing decay.
  \item \textbf{Batch size.} 64 for all architectures.
  \item \textbf{Stopping criterion.} Early stopping with patience~20, monitoring validation macro-F1. Maximum 200 epochs.
  \item \textbf{Loss.} Class-weighted cross-entropy, with per-class weights inversely proportional to class frequency.
  \item \textbf{Input representation.} 1D amplitude matrices using the SenseFi preprocessing pipeline~\cite{yang2023sensefi} for UT-HAR and NTU-Fi HAR.
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility.} Fixed random seed~42 for all indoor benchmark runs; multi-seed variance is reported for target-domain experiments (Section~\ref{sec:multiseed}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Backbone Selection Criteria}
\label{sec:backbonesel}

The backbone forwarded to Stage~III must satisfy three criteria simultaneously:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Stable convergence.} High validation macro-F1 with a small train--validation gap across the indoor benchmarks, indicating reliable learning rather than overfitting.
  \item \textbf{Moderate capacity.} An excessively large model risks overfitting the small WiSe4Car label set (297 events) even after pretraining; model capacity is assessed by parameter count and the degree of overfitting observed on indoor benchmarks.
  \item \textbf{Representational compatibility.} The backbone must be applicable with minimal modification to all three CSI representations (1D, STFT, ACF, Section~\ref{sec:representations}) to enable fair representation comparisons in Stage~III.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Stage~II: Automotive Dataset Construction and Cross-Dataset Alignment}
\label{sec:stage2}

Stage~II constructs the cross-domain experimental setup: WiSe4Car is converted from a passive CSI corpus into an annotated HAR-oriented target dataset, XRF55 is processed into a semantically compatible source domain, and both are aligned to a unified tensor representation suitable for transfer learning.

\subsection{WiSe4Car Dataset Characteristics}
\label{sec:wise4car}

WiSe4Car~\cite{markert2025wise4car} is the first publicly available Wi-Fi CSI dataset collected inside standard vehicle cabins using a commercial Intel Wi-Fi~6 adapter operating at 256 subcarriers. The dataset covers multiple vehicle models, multiple passengers, and both stationary and driving scenarios. Unlike indoor benchmarks, the vehicle cabin is a strongly enclosed metallic environment in which multipath propagation is more complex and hardware-specific phase offsets from the Intel Wi-Fi~6 chipset differ from those of the Atheros and Intel~5300 hardware used in UT-HAR and NTU-Fi HAR.

WiSe4Car was originally collected for passenger height and weight estimation; it provides anonymised video alongside CSI recordings but contains no behavioural activity labels. This gap motivates the annotation step below. The phase-offset characteristics of the Intel Wi-Fi~6 chipset---which introduce larger CFO/SFO distortions than standard 802.11n hardware---also require preprocessing choices that differ from existing indoor datasets.

\subsection{Manual Annotation Procedure}
\label{sec:annotation}

Manual annotation was performed on 75 recording sessions to produce the labelled events required for target-domain HAR evaluation. The annotation process followed three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Session review.} Each session was loaded into a custom timeline viewer that synchronised the CSI amplitude heatmap with the accompanying anonymised video and session metadata (seat configuration, subject identifiers, event timestamps), allowing frame-level inspection of CSI changes alongside visible body motion. Figure~\ref{fig:annotation_tool} shows a representative screenshot of the tool interface.
  \item \textbf{Event labelling.} Behavioural events were assigned to one of six pre-defined classes (Section~\ref{sec:taxonomy}). Each event was marked with start time, end time, subject ID, and class label. Ambiguous transitions, partial occlusions, and events shorter than one second were excluded to preserve annotation quality.
  \item \textbf{Verification and export.} Annotations were cross-checked against session logs and re-examined for temporal boundary accuracy before export as window-level CSV records.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{XRF55 as Source Domain}
\label{sec:xrf55source}

XRF55~\cite{wang2024xrf55} is used as the supervised source domain because it provides a substantially larger and more diverse activity repertoire than UT-HAR and NTU-Fi HAR: 55 daily activities from 39 subjects over 100 days using a 256-subcarrier RF platform. Its role in this thesis is not to provide labels that directly match automotive activities, but to initialise a feature extractor with broad, motion-sensitive CSI representations before target adaptation. The 55 XRF55 classes are mapped to the 6 WiSe4Car classes by semantic grouping (Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy}), yielding a supervised pretraining corpus that shares the target label space. XRF55 is assigned domain identifier domain\_id~$=0$; WiSe4Car is assigned domain\_id~$=1$.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Input Alignment}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

Transfer learning requires source and target inputs to share the same tensor format, even though XRF55 and WiSe4Car differ in hardware platform, effective sampling rate, subcarrier count, and receiver layout. Let $H^{(n)}_t \in \mathbb{C}^{S}$ denote the raw complex CSI vector at time step $t$ on antenna link $n$, where $S$ is the number of subcarriers and $N_L$ is the number of links. The following eight-step pipeline transforms raw CSI into the unified feature tensor $X \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times F}$ with $T = 256$ and $F = 16$.

\paragraph{Step 1: Amplitude extraction.}
Phase is discarded due to the large CFO/SFO offsets inherent in the Intel Wi-Fi~6 chipset~\cite{yousefi2017survey}. The amplitude envelope for subcarrier $k$ on link $n$ is extracted as:
\begin{equation}
  A^{(n)}_t[k] = \left|H^{(n)}_t[k]\right|
               = \sqrt{\operatorname{Re}\!\bigl(H^{(n)}_t[k]\bigr)^2
                      +\operatorname{Im}\!\bigl(H^{(n)}_t[k]\bigr)^2}
\end{equation}

For multi-link setups, per-link amplitudes are concatenated along the subcarrier axis to form a joint amplitude matrix $A_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N_L \cdot S}$.

\paragraph{Step 2: Sliding-window segmentation.}
Each continuous recording is divided into overlapping windows of length $T_w$ samples with hop size $H_w = T_w/2$ (50\% overlap):
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{a}^{(i)}[t,\,k] = A_{t_i + t}[k],\quad t = 0,\ldots,T_w{-}1
\end{equation}
where $t_i = i\cdot H_w$ is the start index of the $i$-th window. The window length $T_w$ is set to cover 4.6~s of data at the native sampling rate.

\paragraph{Step 3: Savitzky-Golay smoothing.}
High-frequency jitter in the amplitude signal is suppressed by a Savitzky-Golay filter of polynomial order $p$ and frame half-length $m$, which fits a local least-squares polynomial and replaces each sample with the corresponding polynomial value:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{a}^{(i)}[t,\,k]
    = \sum_{j=-m}^{m} c_j\;\mathbf{a}^{(i)}[t{+}j,\,k]
\end{equation}
where the convolution coefficients $\{c_j\}$ are derived from the least-squares polynomial fit (degree $p = 3$, half-width $m = 3$, frame length $2m{+}1 = 7$).

\paragraph{Step 4: High-pass Butterworth filtering.}
Quasi-static background drift---arising from static multipath reflections that carry no activity information---is removed by a causal Butterworth high-pass filter of order $n_\text{b}$. Its squared magnitude response is:
\begin{equation}
  |H_\text{HP}(\omega)|^2 = \frac{(\omega/\omega_c)^{2n_\text{b}}}{1 + (\omega/\omega_c)^{2n_\text{b}}}
\end{equation}
A fifth-order filter ($n_\text{b} = 5$) with normalised cut-off frequency $\omega_c = 0.05\,\omega_\text{Nyq}$ is applied independently to each subcarrier series, isolating the activity-related dynamic component from the static environment fingerprint.

\paragraph{Step 5: Demean and linear detrending.}
Residual DC offset is removed by subtracting the per-window temporal mean:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{a}[t,\,k]
    = \hat{a}^{(i)}[t,\,k]
      - \frac{1}{T_w}\sum_{t'=0}^{T_w-1}\hat{a}^{(i)}[t',\,k]
\end{equation}
Any remaining linear trend is then removed by subtracting the least-squares linear fit:
\begin{equation}
  \bar{a}[t,\,k] = \tilde{a}[t,\,k] - \bigl(\hat{\alpha}_k + \hat{\beta}_k\,t\bigr)
\end{equation}
where $(\hat{\alpha}_k,\hat{\beta}_k) = \arg\min_{\alpha,\beta}\sum_{t=0}^{T_w-1}\!\bigl(\tilde{a}[t,k]-\alpha-\beta\,t\bigr)^2$.

\paragraph{Step 6: Temporal resampling.}
Each detrended window is resampled from its native $T_\text{raw}$ time steps to exactly $T = 256$ steps by linear interpolation between adjacent original samples:
\begin{equation}
  x^r[t] = \bar{a}\!\left[s\right](1-\delta) + \bar{a}\!\left[s{+}1\right]\delta,
  \quad s = \left\lfloor\frac{t(T_\text{raw}{-}1)}{T{-}1}\right\rfloor,
  \quad \delta = \frac{t(T_\text{raw}{-}1)}{T{-}1} - s
\end{equation}
This normalises for sampling-rate differences between XRF55 and WiSe4Car while preserving the temporal envelope shape.

\paragraph{Step 7: Subcarrier band pooling.}
The subcarrier dimension is reduced to $F = 16$ sub-bands by partitioning the $S$ subcarriers into $F$ equal-width groups $\{\mathcal{S}_f\}_{f=1}^{F}$ and computing the mean within each group:
\begin{equation}
  B[t,\,f] = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}_f|}\sum_{k\in\mathcal{S}_f} x^r[t,\,k],\quad f = 1,\ldots,F
\end{equation}
This discards fine-grained spectral detail that is hardware-platform-specific while retaining the gross amplitude structure that carries activity information.

\paragraph{Step 8: Feature-wise z-score normalisation.}
Each sub-band channel is standardised to zero mean and unit variance using statistics computed over the training split $\mathcal{T}$:
\begin{equation}
  X[t,\,f] = \frac{B[t,\,f] - \mu_f}{\sigma_f + \varepsilon},\quad
  \mu_f = \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{T}}\!\bigl[B[:,f]\bigr],\quad
  \sigma_f = \operatorname{Std}_{\mathcal{T}}\!\bigl[B[:,f]\bigr]
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon = 10^{-8}$ prevents division by zero. Test-split sub-bands are normalised with training-split statistics to avoid data leakage.

The final aligned tensor $X \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times F} = \mathbb{R}^{256 \times 16}$ is the common input for all model families in Stages~I and III and the base from which the STFT and ACF representations below are derived.

\subsection{CSI Feature Representations: 1D, STFT, and ACF}
\label{sec:representations}

Three complementary representations of the aligned CSI windows are implemented to assess the influence of feature design on cross-domain performance (RQ2). Let $x_f[t] = X[t,f]$ denote the normalised amplitude of sub-band $f$ at time step $t$, for $t = 0,\ldots,T{-}1$ and $f = 1,\ldots,F$ with $T = 256$, $F = 16$.

\paragraph{1D amplitude sequence.}

The 1D representation uses the aligned matrix directly, without further transformation:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{X}_\text{1D} = X \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times F}
\end{equation}

Each row $X[t,:]$ is a snapshot of the 16 band-amplitudes at time $t$; the full matrix preserves the complete temporal evolution of the CSI signal. This representation requires the least computation and is used as the input for all 1D convolutional and recurrent models. Its limitation is sensitivity to absolute amplitude magnitude, which varies with hardware gain settings and environment-specific static multipath.

\paragraph{Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT).}

The STFT decomposes each sub-band time series into local time-frequency components, capturing non-stationary oscillatory motion patterns. For sub-band $f$, the STFT at discrete frequency bin $k$ and time frame $m$ is:
\begin{equation}
  S_f[k,m]
    = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_f[n]\cdot w(n - mH)\cdot e^{-j2\pi kn/N}
\end{equation}

where $w(\cdot)$ is the Hann analysis window of length $N_p$ samples:
\begin{equation}
  w(n) = \frac{1}{2}\!\left(1 - \cos\!\frac{2\pi n}{N_p - 1}\right)\!\cdot\mathbf{1}_{[0,\,N_p{-}1]}(n)
\end{equation}

$H = N_p - N_\text{ov}$ is the hop size between successive frames, and $N$ is the DFT length (set equal to the window length). With the chosen parameters (nperseg $N_p = 64$, noverlap $N_\text{ov} = 48$, nfft $N = 64$, hop $H = 16$) the output dimensions are:
\begin{align}
  K &= \tfrac{N}{2} + 1 = 33 \quad\text{(one-sided frequency bins)} \\
  M &= 1 + \left\lfloor\frac{T - N_p}{H}\right\rfloor
      = 1 + \left\lfloor\frac{256 - 64}{16}\right\rfloor = 13 \quad\text{(time frames)}
\end{align}

Log-magnitude compression is applied to the raw power spectrum to reduce dynamic range and improve gradient flow during training:
\begin{equation}
  X_\text{STFT}[f,\,k,\,m] = \log\!\bigl(1 + |S_f[k,m]|\bigr)
\end{equation}

The resulting per-sample tensor has shape $[F,\,K,\,M] = [16,\,33,\,13]$ and is used as the input for 2D convolutional models that treat the $(K \times M)$ spectrogram as a spatial image. The two datasets differ in their normalisation state: WiSe4Car STFT tensors contain raw power-spectrum values (peak $\approx 4415$) and require additional per-feature z-score normalisation before cross-domain training, whereas XRF55 STFT tensors are produced in a pre-normalised form with values in $[-0.65,\, 3.93]$.

\paragraph{Autocorrelation function (ACF).}

The ACF characterises the temporal self-similarity of the CSI signal and is motivated by its use in DeepCPD~\cite{jayaweera2025deepcpd} for cross-vehicle child presence detection. For sub-band $f$, the biased sample autocorrelation at lag $\tau$ is:
\begin{equation}
  R_f[\tau]
    = \sum_{t=0}^{T-\tau-1} x_f[t]\cdot x_f[t + \tau],\quad \tau = 0,1,\ldots,L
\end{equation}

Each sub-band ACF is then normalised to unit power at lag zero:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{R}_f[\tau] = \frac{R_f[\tau]}{R_f[0]}
\end{equation}

so that $\hat{R}_f[0] = 1$ by construction and $|\hat{R}_f[\tau]| \leq 1$ for all $\tau$. An unbiased correction further divides each lag by $(T - \tau)$; lag~0 is excluded from the output, retaining lags $1,\ldots,L$.

The WiSe4Car representation (termed \emph{ACFplus}) extends the single-branch ACF to a two-branch variant with $V = 2$ output channels:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Branch~1 (raw)}: $\hat{R}_f[\tau]$ computed on the normalised amplitude series $x_f[t]$.
  \item \textbf{Branch~2 (diff)}: $\hat{R}_f^{\Delta}[\tau]$ computed on the first-order difference $\Delta x_f[t] = x_f[t] - x_f[t{-}1]$.
\end{itemize}
The \emph{diff} branch captures velocity-like dynamics (rate of amplitude change), complementing the position-like periodicity in the raw branch. Post-processing applies per-subcarrier z-score, global map z-score, and hard clipping to $[-3,\,3]$. The WiSe4Car per-sample shape is $[2,\,16,\,64]$ ($V{=}2$, $F{=}16$, $L{=}64$). XRF55 uses the single-branch variant ($V{=}1$, $L{=}65$, lag~0 included). When passed to a 2D convolutional encoder, $V$ is treated as the input channel dimension (\texttt{input\_channels=2} for WiSe4Car, \texttt{input\_channels=1} for XRF55).

After normalisation, values lie in $[-3,\,3]$ for both datasets. This sensor-agnostic value range, achieved by construction through lag-0 division, eliminates the amplitude-domain gap between XRF55 (radar, peak ${\approx}4415$ raw STFT power) and WiSe4Car (Wi-Fi~6) without any cross-dataset calibration. It is the primary reason ACFplus achieves the best cross-domain transfer performance in this study.

The key property of the normalised ACF is power invariance: dividing by $R_f[0]$ makes $\hat{R}_f$ insensitive to the absolute amplitude level, which varies with hardware gain and vehicle-specific static reflections. By the Wiener-Khinchin theorem, the ACF and the power spectral density (PSD) form a Fourier pair:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{S}_f(\omega) = \sum_{\tau=-\infty}^{\infty} R_f[\tau]\,e^{-j\omega\tau}
\end{equation}

The ACF therefore encodes the same frequency-domain information as the PSD while avoiding the window-length sensitivity of the STFT---a particularly valuable property for WiSe4Car events, whose duration spans a wide range (1--10~s).

\section{Stage~III: Transfer Learning and Domain Adaptation}
\label{sec:transfer}

Stage~III systematically evaluates how knowledge from the XRF55 source domain can be reused for WiSe4Car target classification. Four experimental settings are compared to isolate the contribution of each transfer component.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}

Let $\mathcal{D}_s = \{(x_i^s, y_i^s)\}_{i=1}^{n_s}$ denote the labelled XRF55 source domain and $\mathcal{D}_t^l = \{(x_j^t, y_j^t)\}_{j=1}^{n_t^l}$ the labelled WiSe4Car target set ($n_t^l = 297$). The unlabelled WiSe4Car recording pool $\mathcal{D}_t^u = \{x_k^t\}_{k=1}^{n_t^u}$ provides additional target-distribution signal without activity annotations. The encoder $f_\theta$ maps inputs to a latent representation; the classifier $g_\phi$ maps representations to activity labels. Both source and target share the same $C = 6$ output classes (Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy}). The objective is to learn $(f_\theta, g_\phi)$ that achieves high macro-F1 on $\mathcal{D}_t^l$ while reducing dependence on the small labelled target set.

\subsection{Setting~A: Scratch Target Training}
\label{sec:scratch}

The scratch baseline trains the encoder and classifier directly on $\mathcal{D}_t^l$ without any source data or unlabelled adaptation. This setting provides the lower-bound performance achievable from target labels alone and is the reference against which all transfer strategies are measured.

\subsection{Setting~B: Supervised Source Pretraining and Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:supervisedtransfer}

The encoder $f_\theta$ is first trained with full supervision on $\mathcal{D}_s$ using the 6-class XRF55 mapping. Only $f_\theta$ is transferred; the source classifier head is discarded and replaced with a WiSe4Car-specific classifier $g_\phi$. For fine-tuning on $\mathcal{D}_t^l$, early layers of $f_\theta$ are frozen for the first 50 epochs to prevent catastrophic forgetting; all layers are subsequently unfrozen. This setting isolates the contribution of source pretraining without any unsupervised target-domain signal.

\subsection{Setting~C: Self-Supervised Target Adaptation with BYOL}
\label{sec:ssl_adapt}

After source pretraining, the encoder $f_\theta$ is adapted to the target distribution using the unlabelled pool $\mathcal{D}_t^u$ before supervised fine-tuning. The adaptation objective is Bootstrap Your Own Latent (BYOL)~\cite{grill2020byol}, a self-supervised method that requires no negative sample pairs---a critical property for the WiSe4Car setting where the labelled pool contains only 297 events and batch sizes are limited to 32.

\paragraph{Network architecture.}
BYOL maintains two networks with identical architecture but distinct parameter sets. The \emph{online network} consists of the encoder $f_\theta$, a projector $g_\theta$, and a predictor $q_\theta$. The \emph{target network} consists of a momentum-updated encoder $f_\xi$ and projector $g_\xi$, but \emph{no} predictor. The asymmetry---predictor on the online side only---is key to preventing representational collapse without negative pairs~\cite{grill2020byol}. Both projector networks have a two-layer MLP architecture with hidden dimension 256 and output dimension 128; the predictor shares the same structure.

\paragraph{Training objective.}
Given an unlabelled CSI window $x \in \mathcal{D}_t^u$, two augmented views $v^A = t(x)$ and $v^B = t'(x)$ are independently sampled. The online network produces projected representations and predictions:
\begin{align}
  z_\theta^A &= g_\theta\!\bigl(f_\theta(v^A)\bigr), &
  p_\theta^A &= q_\theta\!\bigl(z_\theta^A\bigr)
\end{align}
The target network produces stop-gradient projections:
\begin{align}
  z_\xi^A &= \mathrm{sg}\!\bigl(g_\xi(f_\xi(v^A))\bigr), &
  z_\xi^B &= \mathrm{sg}\!\bigl(g_\xi(f_\xi(v^B))\bigr)
\end{align}
where $\mathrm{sg}(\cdot)$ denotes the stop-gradient operation, blocking all gradient flow through the target branch. The per-sample loss is the symmetrised negative cosine similarity:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{BYOL}
    = -\frac{p_\theta^A}{\|p_\theta^A\|_2} \cdot \frac{z_\xi^B}{\|z_\xi^B\|_2}
      -\frac{p_\theta^B}{\|p_\theta^B\|_2} \cdot \frac{z_\xi^A}{\|z_\xi^A\|_2}
\label{eq:byol}
\end{equation}
Minimising Equation~\eqref{eq:byol} drives the online predictor output to match the target projector output for the same input under two different augmentations.

\paragraph{Target network update.}
The target network parameters $\xi$ are never updated by gradient descent. Instead, they follow an exponential moving average (EMA) of the online parameters $\theta$:
\begin{equation}
  \xi \leftarrow \tau\,\xi + (1 - \tau)\,\theta
\end{equation}
with momentum $\tau = 0.996$. The slow-moving target provides stable regression targets, preventing the online network from collapsing to a trivial constant solution.

\paragraph{CSI-specific augmentations.}
Six augmentations are applied independently to each view, designed to preserve the class-relevant temporal structure of CSI signals while introducing sufficient variation to force invariant representations:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Gaussian noise} ($\sigma = 0.01$): low-amplitude additive noise simulating hardware thermal noise without distorting motion-related amplitude patterns.
  \item \textbf{Time mask}: a random contiguous segment of 10\,\% of the time axis is zeroed, promoting robustness to brief temporal occlusions.
  \item \textbf{Subcarrier mask}: a random 10\,\% of sub-bands are set to zero, simulating narrowband interference or selective subcarrier dropout.
  \item \textbf{Time shift}: circular shift along the time axis by a random offset of up to $\pm 10\,\%$ of the window length, introducing temporal translation invariance.
  \item \textbf{Amplitude scaling}: global multiplicative scaling by a factor drawn uniformly from $[0.8,\, 1.2]$, simulating inter-session AGC gain variation.
  \item \textbf{Local crop and resize}: a random contiguous segment of 80\,\% of the window is cropped and linearly resampled back to $T = 256$ steps, introducing invariance to activity speed variation.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Training data and method selection rationale.}
BYOL pre-training uses the XRF55 source corpus (1{,}584 train / 198 val / 198 test windows) \emph{without} any activity labels for 30 epochs with AdamW ($\mathrm{lr} = 3\!\times\!10^{-4}$, $\mathrm{wd} = 10^{-4}$), batch size 32. After pre-training, the classification head is discarded; only the encoder weights $f_\theta$ are retained and used as initialisation for supervised fine-tuning on WiSe4Car.

BYOL is chosen over contrastive alternatives---SimCLR~\cite{chen2020simclr} and MoCo~\cite{he2020moco}---because both require large negative sets (SimCLR: batch $\geq 4096$; MoCo: memory queue of 65536 keys) that are infeasible at batch size 32. Additionally, the six activity classes share low-level motion statistics (e.g., \textit{sitting} and \textit{bending} both involve slow torso motion), making randomly sampled negatives semantically unreliable. BYOL avoids both problems by bootstrapping entirely from positive pairs~\cite{grill2020byol}.

\subsection{Setting~D: Distribution Alignment with MMD, DRCA, and DANN}
\label{sec:domainadapt}

Domain adaptation methods explicitly minimise the divergence between source and target feature distributions during joint training on $\mathcal{D}_s$ and $\mathcal{D}_t^u$. Three objectives of increasing structural complexity are evaluated, ordered by their inductive assumptions about the nature of the domain shift.

\paragraph{Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD).}
MMD minimises the kernel mean embedding distance between source and target distributions in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{MMD} =
  \left\|\frac{1}{n_s}\sum_{i=1}^{n_s}\phi\bigl(f_\theta(x_i^s)\bigr)
        -\frac{1}{n_t^u}\sum_{j=1}^{n_t^u}\phi\bigl(f_\theta(x_j^t)\bigr)
  \right\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2
\end{equation}
where $\phi:\mathbb{R}^d\!\to\!\mathcal{H}$ is the feature map induced by the RBF kernel $k(z,z')=\exp(-\|z{-}z'\|^2/2\sigma^2)$. MMD aligns the first-order kernel mean embedding of the feature distributions; it does not enforce explicit covariance alignment. The total loss is $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\text{CE} + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_\text{MMD}$, with $\lambda$ tuned on the validation split.

\paragraph{Domain Regularized Component Analysis (DRCA)~\cite{zhan2024drca}.}
DRCA finds a linear projection $W \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times d}$ that maps encoder features into a $d$-dimensional subspace by simultaneously minimising between-domain scatter and preserving within-domain scatter~\cite{zhan2024drca}. Let $\boldsymbol{\mu}_s, \boldsymbol{\mu}_t \in \mathbb{R}^D$ denote the source and target domain means, and $\Sigma_s, \Sigma_t \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$ their empirical covariance matrices. The between-domain scatter matrix $S_b$ and pooled within-domain scatter matrix $S_w$ are:
\begin{align}
  S_b &= (\boldsymbol{\mu}_s - \boldsymbol{\mu}_t)(\boldsymbol{\mu}_s - \boldsymbol{\mu}_t)^\top \\
  S_w &= \Sigma_s + \Sigma_t
\end{align}
The projection $W$ is obtained by maximising the Rayleigh quotient $J(W) = W^\top S_b W \,/\, W^\top S_w W$, which reduces to the generalised eigenvalue problem:
\begin{equation}
  S_b\,W = S_w\,W\,\Lambda
\label{eq:drca}
\end{equation}
The columns of $W$ are the $d$ eigenvectors corresponding to the $d$ largest eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_d$. Features are projected as $z = W^\top x$ before training, so the encoder is trained and applied in the domain-aligned subspace without requiring any modification to the loss function. DRCA differs from MMD and DANN in being a pre-processing projection rather than a regularisation term added to the training objective: it adapts the feature space once, after which standard cross-entropy training proceeds on the projected features.

\paragraph{Domain-Adversarial Neural Networks (DANN)~\cite{ganin2016domain}.}
DANN adopts an adversarial formulation: a domain discriminator $h_\psi$ is trained to distinguish source from target feature vectors, while the encoder $f_\theta$ is simultaneously trained to confuse it. A gradient reversal layer (GRL) inserted between $f_\theta$ and $h_\psi$ negates the discriminator gradient during backpropagation, thereby pushing $f_\theta$ toward domain-invariant representations while preserving task-discriminative information:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\text{CE}(g_\phi \circ f_\theta;\,\mathcal{D}_s)
               - \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_\text{adv}\!\left(h_\psi \circ \mathrm{GRL}(f_\theta);\,\mathcal{D}_s \cup \mathcal{D}_t^u\right)
\end{equation}
The GRL affects only the discriminator pathway; gradients from the task loss reach $f_\theta$ unmodified. Unlike MMD and DRCA, which impose explicit distributional distances, DANN implicitly minimises a divergence upper-bounded by the $\mathcal{H}\Delta\mathcal{H}$-divergence~\cite{ben2010theory}, granting the model greater flexibility to handle non-Gaussian and multi-modal domain shifts.

\section{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

Macro-averaged F1 (macro-F1) is the primary metric for WiSe4Car evaluation because it gives equal weight to each class and is more informative than accuracy under the observed class imbalance (Table~\ref{tab:classdist_ch4}). Per-class recall is reported alongside macro-F1 to identify which activity classes benefit most from transfer. For indoor benchmarks, top-1 accuracy is reported to facilitate comparison with prior work~\cite{yang2023sensefi}.

\subsection{Pooled Evaluation}
\label{sec:pooled}

Pooled evaluation merges all 6 vehicles and splits the 2{,}117 WiSe4Car windows into train / validation / test partitions using stratified random sampling to preserve class proportions: 80\,\% train (1{,}695 windows), 10\,\% validation (211), and 10\,\% test (211). This protocol measures average performance under the assumption that train and test samples are drawn i.i.d.\ from the same vehicle-pooled distribution and provides the primary reference for cross-method comparisons.

\subsection{In-Car Cross-Validation}
\label{sec:incar}

In-car evaluation uses 6-fold cross-validation where each fold designates one vehicle as the test set and trains on the remaining five. The validation set is drawn from the training cars (10\,\% of training windows), keeping the test car entirely unseen during training. Macro-F1 is averaged across all six folds. This protocol measures within-distribution generalisation when train and test sessions belong to different recordings of the same vehicle fleet, with partial overlap in per-vehicle channel statistics.

\subsection{Leave-One-Car-Out Evaluation}
\label{sec:loco}

Leave-one-car-out (LOCO) evaluation is structurally identical to In-Car in that each fold holds out one vehicle, but differs in that the validation set is also restricted to training cars. This protocol measures strict cross-vehicle generalisation---the test car is completely unseen during all phases of training and model selection. LOCO is the hardest protocol due to per-vehicle class coverage gaps: \textit{waving} (2 total samples) and \textit{bending} (absent in Car~3) produce near-zero per-class recall in affected folds regardless of model quality, creating a structural ceiling on 6-class LOCO Macro-F1. A restricted 3-class variant (Sitting / Turning / Using Phone, Cars~1,3,4,5,6) is reported separately to isolate algorithm performance from annotation coverage.

\subsection{Ablation Study Design}
\label{sec:ablation_design}

Ablation experiments isolate individual pipeline components by disabling one factor at a time while holding all others fixed. The ablation dimensions are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Representation:} 1D vs.\ STFT vs.\ ACF (same model and setting).
  \item \textbf{Source pretraining:} Setting~A vs.\ Setting~B across backbone choices.
  \item \textbf{SSL adaptation:} Setting~B (no SSL) vs.\ Setting~C (with SSL).
  \item \textbf{Domain adaptation method:} MMD vs.\ DRCA vs.\ DANN within Setting~D.
  \item \textbf{Fine-tuning strategy:} frozen encoder vs.\ full fine-tune vs.\ gradual unfreezing.
  \item \textbf{Class imbalance handling:} no reweighting vs.\ weighted cross-entropy vs.\ minority oversampling.
\end{itemize}

\section{WiSe4Car Annotation Protocol}
\label{sec:annotation_protocol}

The WiSe4Car dataset was annotated to produce the labelled events required for in-cabin HAR evaluation. The annotation process comprised three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Session review.} Each of the 75 recording sessions was loaded into a custom timeline viewer synchronising the CSI amplitude heatmap with the accompanying session metadata (seat configuration, subject identifiers, timestamps). Frame-level markers were examined to identify distinct behavioural events.
  \item \textbf{Event labelling.} Annotated events were assigned to one of six behaviour classes defined \textit{a priori} based on inspection of the dataset content: \textit{sitting} (deliberate postural adjustment or sustained static pose), \textit{reaching} (forward or lateral arm extension), \textit{turning} (upper-body rotation towards a different seat zone), \textit{bending} (forward torso flexion), \textit{waving} (hand wave gesture), and \textit{using phone} (hand-held device interaction). The integer label identifiers are listed in Table~\ref{tab:labelmap}.
  \item \textbf{Verification and export.} Each annotated event was cross-checked against the session log. Ambiguous clips (transition events, partial occlusions) were excluded. Verified annotations were exported as window-level CSV files containing start time, duration, subject ID, and class label, yielding 297 labelled events over 75 sessions.
\end{enumerate}

\section{CSI Feature Representation Design}
\label{sec:reprdesign}

To assess the influence of feature representation on cross-domain performance (RQ2), three complementary representations of the unified CSI windows ($X \in \mathbb{R}^{256 \times 16}$) were implemented:

\paragraph{1D amplitude sequence.} The $[256, 16]$ matrix is transposed to channels-first layout $[16, 256]$ matching PyTorch 1D convolution convention. No spectral transformation is applied; the representation preserves the full temporal dynamics and serves as the baseline for the MultiscaleCNN model (\texttt{input\_channels=16}).

\paragraph{Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT).} Per-band STFT with a Hann window of length 64 samples (nperseg~$= 64$, noverlap~$= 48$, nfft~$= 64$) is applied independently to each of the 16 sub-bands. This produces a time-frequency tensor of shape $[16,\,33,\,13]$ per sample after log$_{1+}$ compression. The 33 one-sided frequency bins span 0--500~Hz at the 1~kHz CSI sampling rate, and the 13 time frames cover the full 256-sample window. WiSe4Car STFT tensors carry raw power-spectrum values (peak $\approx 4415$) and are z-score normalised per feature before cross-domain training; XRF55 tensors are delivered pre-normalised in $[-0.65,\, 3.93]$.

\paragraph{ACFplus.} The two-branch unbiased ACF (raw + diff, lags 1--64) is computed and clipped to $[-3,\,3]$ as described in Section~\ref{sec:representations}, yielding shape $[2,\,16,\,64]$ per WiSe4Car sample. The two-channel map is fed to the ResNet2D encoder with \texttt{input\_channels=2}. Because the lag-0 normalisation makes ACFplus values sensor-agnostic by construction, no additional cross-dataset normalisation is required for the XRF55~$\to$~WiSe4Car transfer setting---the primary reason this representation outperforms STFT on all domain-adaptation protocols~\cite{jayaweera2025deepcpd}.

\section{XRF55 Source Pre-training and Architecture Search}
\label{sec:xrf55pretrain}

\subsection{Preprocessing and Label Mapping}

XRF55 was preprocessed through the unified pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}) to produce features of shape $[N,\,256,\,16]$. The original 55 activity classes were mapped to the 6 WiSe4Car behaviour classes by semantic grouping: for example, ``sitting down'', ``adjusting seat'', and ``cross-legged sitting'' were all mapped to \textit{sitting}; ``reaching for an object'' and ``picking up'' were mapped to \textit{reaching}. The resulting 6-class XRF55 training set provides the supervised pre-training corpus for the transfer pipeline.

\subsection{Stage~A: Backbone Width Search}

A plain 1D convolutional encoder with a depth ladder $\{1\times,\,2\times,\,4\times,\,8\times\}$ relative to a base width $w$ was trained for each candidate $w \in \{64,\,96\}$ with fixed num\_blocks~$= 4$, Adam at $10^{-3}$, cosine learning rate annealing, and early stopping (patience~10, minimum 20 epochs). The search objective was top-1 validation accuracy on the held-out XRF55 split.

\subsection{Stage~B: Architecture Family Comparison}

Using the best Stage~A configuration ($w = 96$), three architectural families were each evaluated in three independent trials with different random seeds: (i)~\textbf{plain} residual-free convolutional stacks; (ii)~\textbf{ResNet-style} blocks with skip connections; and (iii)~\textbf{multiscale} parallel-path encoders that process the input at three temporal resolutions simultaneously and fuse representations at the bottleneck. Each trial ran for a maximum of 140 epochs with cosine decay; the best validation accuracy and macro-averaged F1 score were recorded per trial.

\section{Model Architectures}
\label{sec:architectures}

Two encoder architectures are used depending on the input representation. Both are trained with AdamW ($\mathrm{wd} = 10^{-4}$, gradient clipping 1.0) and early stopping on validation Macro-F1.

\paragraph{MultiscaleCNN (1D representation).}
A multi-branch 1D CNN that processes the $[16, 256]$ input through four parallel convolutional pathways with kernel sizes $\{3, 7, 15, 31\}$, capturing temporal patterns at four distinct scales. Branch outputs are concatenated and reduced via global average pooling to a fixed-length feature vector, followed by a dropout layer (rate~0.3) and a fully connected classification head. Total parameters: \textbf{3.65~M} (14.0~MB FP32).

\paragraph{ResNet2D (STFT and ACFplus).}
A lightweight 2D residual network adapted for CSI spectral inputs. Residual blocks use batch normalisation and GELU activations; global average pooling produces the feature vector; dropout rate is 0.2. Input channels are representation-dependent: 16 for STFT, 2 for ACFplus. Total parameters: \textbf{2.80~M} (ACFplus, 10.7~MB) and \textbf{2.82~M} (STFT, 10.8~MB FP32). The DANN domain discriminator appends a two-layer MLP ($\mathrm{FC}(128) \to \mathrm{BN} \to \mathrm{ReLU} \to \mathrm{Dropout}(0.2) \to \mathrm{FC}(2)$) connected via the gradient reversal layer ($\lambda = 1.0$).

\begin{figure}[!ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/Resnet2D_Model_Architecture.png}
  \caption{ResNet2D architecture used for STFT and ACFplus representations. The stem applies a $7\!\times\!7$ convolution followed by batch normalisation and max pooling. Four residual stages progressively double the channel width ($C\!=\!64\!\to\!128\!\to\!256\!\to\!512$), each containing two $3\!\times\!3$ residual blocks with skip connections. The classification head uses global average pooling, dropout ($p\!=\!0.2$), and a fully connected layer mapping to the 6 output classes. For the STFT input the tensor shape entering the stem is $[16, 33, 13]$; for ACFplus it is $[2, 16, 64]$.}

\end{figure}

\section{Transfer Strategy Implementation}
\label{sec:threestage}

Four strategies are evaluated independently for each of the three representations. All strategies use the same model architectures, optimizer (AdamW, $\mathrm{lr}=3\!\times\!10^{-4}$ for pre-training, $\mathrm{lr}=2\!\times\!10^{-4}$ for fine-tuning), batch size 32, and inverse-frequency weighted sampler. Label smoothing (0.05) and gradient clipping (1.0) are applied throughout.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/systemmodel.png}
  \caption{Overview of the complete transfer learning pipeline. Left: the three CSI feature representations (1D amplitude, STFT, ACFplus) serve as encoder inputs. Stage~1 (SSL Pre-training): BYOL trains the encoder on XRF55 without labels, using the online/target network pair and CSI augmentations. Stage~2 (Supervised Transfer): the encoder is pre-trained on the 6-class XRF55 labels, then fine-tuned on WiSe4Car. Stage~3 (DANN Fine-tuning): a gradient reversal layer aligns the XRF55 and WiSe4Car feature distributions during joint training before a final labelled fine-tuning step. Stage~4: the adapted encoder is deployed for inference on WiSe4Car test data.}
  \label{fig:system_overview}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Baseline.} The encoder and classifier are randomly initialised and trained directly on WiSe4Car using cross-entropy loss with label smoothing 0.05. Early stopping patience is 15 epochs with a 60-epoch cap. This is the lower-bound reference.

\paragraph{SSL (BYOL pre-training).} Stage~1: BYOL trains the encoder on XRF55 (1{,}584 samples) for 30 epochs without using any labels. The projector and predictor are discarded. Stage~2: the pre-trained encoder is fine-tuned end-to-end on WiSe4Car for up to 40 epochs (patience 10) using cross-entropy with label smoothing 0.05.

\paragraph{Transfer (supervised pre-training).} Stage~1: standard supervised training on the 6-class XRF55 split (30 epochs, patience 8, label smoothing 0.02). The XRF55 classification head is replaced with a randomly initialised WiSe4Car head. Stage~2: full end-to-end fine-tuning on WiSe4Car, identical hyperparameters to SSL Stage~2.

\paragraph{DA Transfer.} Stage~1 is identical to Transfer Stage~1. Stage~2 performs domain adaptation jointly on XRF55 (source, with labels) and WiSe4Car (target, unlabelled) for 20 epochs (patience 5). Three DA methods are evaluated: DANN ($\lambda = 1.0$), MMD (RBF kernel, weight 1.0), and DRCA (covariance projection). Stage~3: fine-tunes the domain-adapted encoder on labelled WiSe4Car (40 epochs, patience 10) using focal loss ($\gamma = 2.0$) + label smoothing 0.02 with a capped inverse-frequency sampler (cap\_ratio~$= 5.0$). The focal loss down-weights easy examples, improving recall for the minority classes (Bending, Reaching).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion_ch4}

\subsection{Addressing RQ1: Feasibility Under Realistic Conditions}

The indoor benchmark results confirm that Wi-Fi CSI sensing achieves high accuracy in controlled laboratory conditions: LeNet and CNN+GRU reach 97\%+ on both UT-HAR and NTU-Fi HAR with train--test gaps below 3~pp. The results establish that the sensing modality is technically feasible for human activity recognition when hardware, environment, and activity classes are consistent between training and deployment.

However, two observations underline the challenges for automotive deployment. First, the 2{,}117-sample WiSe4Car dataset is severely imbalanced (Waving: 2 windows, Bending: 72), and per-vehicle coverage gaps create a structural ceiling on LOCO performance that is independent of model capacity. Second, the domain gap between XRF55 (radar, open laboratory) and WiSe4Car (Wi-Fi~6, enclosed vehicle cabin) is large enough that unadapted transfer (ACFplus Baseline: 0.491 pooled Macro-F1) leaves substantial room for improvement.

\subsection{Addressing RQ2: Design Choices That Determine Performance}

Four findings from the completed experiments directly address RQ2:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Representation is the dominant design factor.} ACFplus consistently outperforms STFT and 1D across all strategies and protocols (Table~\ref{tab:transfer_full}). The key differentiator is sensor-agnostic normalisation: lag-0 division makes ACFplus values independent of absolute amplitude, cancelling the hardware-specific scale mismatch between radar and Wi-Fi CSI. No comparable cross-dataset normalisation achieves this for STFT.

  \item \textbf{DA Transfer consistently improves over unadapted pre-training.} For ACFplus, DANN domain adaptation adds $+$0.241 pooled Macro-F1 over supervised Transfer (0.521 $\to$ 0.762). Even for STFT and 1D, DA Transfer is the top strategy, confirming that explicit distribution alignment provides value beyond structural invariance.

  \item \textbf{SSL (BYOL) underperforms supervised Transfer when source labels are available.} BYOL reaches 0.513 pooled Macro-F1 on ACFplus vs.\ Transfer's 0.521. The exception is 1D, where BYOL (0.401) improves over Baseline (0.371) despite underperforming Transfer (0.421). For STFT, BYOL (0.446) actually falls below Baseline (0.458), consistent with BYOL augmentations distorting spectral structure on frequency-domain inputs.

  \item \textbf{Architecture family matters for source pre-training quality.} On XRF55, the multiscale encoder (val\_acc~0.9343, macro F1~0.9268) outperforms plain and ResNet families by 3.5~pp and 0.7~pp respectively, confirming that multi-resolution temporal receptive fields---not raw parameter count---drive discriminative representation quality for 6-class activity recognition.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}
%% ───────────────────────────────────────

\section{Addressing RQ1: Feasibility Under Realistic Conditions}
\label{sec:rq1}

The benchmark results confirm that Wi-Fi CSI sensing is technically feasible for HAR in controlled laboratory conditions, reaching $>97\%$ top-1 accuracy on UT-HAR and NTU-Fi HAR with compact models (LeNet, CNN+GRU). However, performance degrades substantially under cross-domain conditions. In the automotive setting, the ACFplus~+~DA Transfer pipeline reaches pooled Macro-F1~$=$~0.762 on five-class WiSe4Car evaluation---a meaningful result demonstrating feasibility for basic in-cabin activity recognition, but still significantly below laboratory-condition benchmarks. The LOCO Macro-F1 of 0.716 further confirms that cross-vehicle generalisation is achievable, though annotation coverage gaps for minority classes (Waving, Bending) prevent reliable six-class evaluation across all car folds.

\section{Addressing RQ2: Design Choices That Matter}
\label{sec:rq2}

The results yield four clear design conclusions (Section~\ref{sec:discussion_ch4}). Feature representation is the most impactful factor: ACFplus outperforms STFT by $+$0.196 and 1D by $+$0.230 pooled Macro-F1 under DA Transfer, because lag-0 normalisation eliminates the amplitude-domain gap by construction. Domain adaptation (DANN) is the second most impactful factor, adding $+$0.241 over unadapted Transfer for ACFplus. The encoder architecture matters for source pre-training quality (multiscale $+$3.5~pp over plain on XRF55), but architecture choice has smaller impact than representation choice for final target-domain performance. Self-supervised BYOL pre-training does not improve over supervised Transfer when source labels are available at the current dataset scale (XRF55: 1{,}584 training windows).

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations constrain the conclusions. First, the WiSe4Car evaluation uses 2{,}117 windows from 75 sessions; the severe class imbalance (Waving: 2 samples, Bending: 72 samples) and per-vehicle coverage gaps make six-class LOCO evaluation structurally unreliable---the 3-class LOCO (0.767) is a more interpretable bound. Second, the XRF55 source domain is radar-based, introducing a sensor-modality gap that standard domain adaptation methods only partially bridge. Third, all evaluation is offline; real-time deployment would require streaming segmentation and sub-100~ms inference. Fourth, the label taxonomy was defined during annotation and may not cover all safety-critical in-vehicle activities (e.g., child presence, occupant restraint). All models satisfy edge-deployment constraints ($\leq$2~ms GPU inference, $\leq$11~MB model size), but embedded automotive hardware validation remains outstanding.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

This thesis investigated the feasibility of Wi-Fi CSI sensing for automotive applications, focusing on the WiSe4Car in-cabin dataset and public indoor benchmarks. The main conclusions are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Wi-Fi CSI sensing achieves $>97\%$ accuracy on standard indoor HAR benchmarks under matched train-test conditions. Performance drops substantially under domain shift, confirming that cross-domain generalisation is the primary technical challenge for automotive deployment.
  \item ACFplus (two-branch unbiased autocorrelation: raw~+~diff) is the most domain-robust representation. Its lag-0 normalisation cancels sensor-specific amplitude scale by construction, outperforming STFT and 1D raw amplitude by up to $+$0.230 Macro-F1 under DA Transfer.
  \item DA Transfer (supervised XRF55 pre-training $\to$ DANN alignment $\to$ WiSe4Car fine-tuning) is the best strategy for all representations, achieving pooled Macro-F1~$=$~0.762, in-car Macro-F1~$=$~0.809, and LOCO Macro-F1~$=$~0.716 on five-class WiSe4Car evaluation.
  \item Supervised pre-training outperforms self-supervised BYOL pre-training when source labels are available. BYOL provides no advantage over supervised Transfer at the current scale (XRF55: 1{,}584 training samples).
  \item All deployed models satisfy edge-deployment constraints: $\leq$2~ms GPU inference latency per sample and $\leq$11~MB model size (FP32), making real-time in-vehicle HAR technically feasible.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:futurework}

Several directions are identified for future research:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Larger and more balanced WiSe4Car annotation.} The current annotation (2{,}117 windows from 297 events) is sufficient for pooled evaluation but too sparse for minority classes in LOCO. Expanding coverage for Waving, Bending, and Reaching---particularly per-vehicle---would lift the structural LOCO ceiling and enable reliable six-class cross-vehicle evaluation.
  \item \textbf{LLM-enhanced zero-shot inference.} Recent work on Wi-Chat~\cite{zhang2025wichat} demonstrates that large language models can interpret CSI patterns in a zero-shot manner by incorporating Wi-Fi sensing physics into prompts, achieving up to 90\% activity recognition accuracy without any labelled training data. This paradigm warrants evaluation in the automotive context, where labelled data is scarce and LLM-based inference could eliminate the need for the annotation effort required by conventional supervised approaches.
  \item \textbf{IEEE~802.11bf standardisation.} The emerging WLAN sensing standard~\cite{du2025overview} will enable native CSI extraction on future Wi-Fi devices, lowering the barrier to deployment.
  \item \textbf{Real-time edge deployment.} Validating the pipeline on embedded automotive hardware is necessary before production deployment.
  \item \textbf{Multi-modal fusion.} Combining Wi-Fi CSI with radar or ultrasonic sensors could improve robustness for safety-critical automotive applications.
\end{itemize}
